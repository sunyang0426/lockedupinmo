Missouri’s state prison system is facing two major challenges today: overcrowding and increase of aging inmates. These two inner-related problems haven’t been driven by a rapid growth of crime, but by the encouragement on tougher sentencing policies from the state government.

In 1979, Missouri state government approved the Capital Murder Statute (section 565.008 RSMo) which required an offender to serve at least 50 years prior to being eligible for parole release. The section was replaced in 1985 by a new Murder First Degree Statute (section 565.020 RSMo), which prohibited parole release.

Because of tougher law on sentencing, Missouri is witnessing a larger group of people locked up in the prisons and getting old behind the bars.

By Yang Sun, Columbia Missourian | May, 2017

State Prisons in Missouri

There are 21 state prisons in Missouri operated by Department of Corrections. The entire system can hold 32765 inmates. According to Division of Adult Institutions Formal Director Dave Dormire, in the summer of 2016 the DOC had roughly 33,900 inmates, making for even more cramped conditions than what the system was previously experiencing.

The growth of state prisons' inmate population

The inmate population within the state prison system has set on an rollercoaster with numbers going up and down each year. The overall population has increased drastically from 1979 to 2015, the total has grown by more than five times.

Facing the overcrowding of the prisons, Michael Wolff,former Missouri Supreme Court judge and chief justice, believes that building new prisons is the worst solution. More people will end up in prisons, which causing a vicious circle.

Department of Corrections’ new Director Anne Precythe claimed that she’s committed to not building another prison. She said the department would instead look to reduce prison populations before expanding the system.

The norm in prisons is to use 55-and-older as the metric associated with senior prisoners, much younger than the average. Most inmates had abused drugs and alcohol or neglected their health for years.

People sent to prison are generally less healthy than the general population. Chronic diseases are commonly found among senior inmates.

According to a Pew report, health care for older prisoners costs between four and eight times what it does for younger prisoners.

Percentage change by age group (From 2008 to 2015)

The inmates above age 55 was the fastest growthing age group. This age group also takes more percentage among the total population.

Missouri's approaches

Missouri has taken several steps to address its growing geriatric prisoner population. However, the rise of inmates population and amount of geriatric prisoners has caused an increase of Department of Corrections’ budget by over 30% from 1999 to 2015.
